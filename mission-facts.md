## Apollo 11 Summary

Apollo 11 was the first spaceflight that landed humans on the Moon. Mission commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin formed the American crew that landed the Apollo Lunar Module Eagle on July 20, 1969, at 20:17 UTC. Armstrong became the first person to step onto the lunar surface six hours and 39 minutes later on July 21 at 02:56 UTC; Aldrin joined him about 20 minutes later. They spent about two and a quarter hours together outside the spacecraft, and collected 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of lunar material to bring back to Earth. Command module pilot Michael Collins flew the command module Columbia alone in lunar orbit while they were on the Moon's surface. Armstrong and Aldrin took two and a half hours to reach Columbia. The crew returned to Earth with a splashdown on July 24, 1969. Apollo 11 effectively ended the Space Race and fulfilled a national goal proposed in 1961 by President John F. Kennedy: "before this decade is out, of landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth."

The Saturn V rocket for Apollo 11 was constructed at the Marshall Space Flight Center and tested at the Mississippi Test Facility, with final assembly at Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39. It launched on July 16, 1969, at 13:32 UTC, and reached the Moon after a three-day translunar coast. The mission lasted 8 days, 3 hours, 18 minutes, 35 seconds from launch to splashdown.

## Moon Facts

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite. Its diameter is about one-quarter that of Earth (3,474 km), making it the fifth-largest moon in the Solar System. It is the largest natural satellite of a terrestrial planet and is the second-densest moon among those whose density is known. The Moon is in synchronous rotation with Earth, always showing the same face; the far side is not visible from Earth. Its average distance from Earth is 384,400 km.

The Moon has no atmosphere, no magnetic field, and no life. It influences Earth's tides due to gravitational interaction. The surface is covered in regolith and marked by impact craters, maria (basaltic plains), and highlands.
